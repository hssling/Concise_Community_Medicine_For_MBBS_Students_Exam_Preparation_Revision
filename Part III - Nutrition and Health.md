## Section A: Basic Nutrition Concepts

### 1. Macronutrients

#### Carbohydrates
- **Function**: Primary energy source (4 kcal/g)
- **Classification**: 
  - Simple sugars (glucose, fructose)
  - Complex carbohydrates (starch, cellulose)
- **Daily requirement**: 50-60% of total calories

**MCQ Point**: Excess carbohydrate intake is converted to fat and stored in adipose tissue.

#### Proteins
- **Function**: Building and repair (4 kcal/g)
- **Essential amino acids**: 9 out of 20
- **High biological value**: Animal proteins, pulses
- **Daily requirement**: 0.8-1.0 g/kg body weight

**Memory Aid**: "PASH" - Proteins for Amino acids, Structure, Hormones

#### Fats
- **Function**: Energy storage (9 kcal/g)
- **Essential fatty acids**: Linoleic acid, α-linolenic acid
- **Daily requirement**: 20-30% of total calories
- **Saturated fats**: <10% of total calories

### 2. Micronutrients

#### Vitamins (Fat-soluble)
| Vitamin | Primary Function | Deficiency Manifestation | RDA |
|---------|------------------|--------------------------|-----|
| A | Vision, immunity | Night blindness, xerophthalmia | 600 μg |
| D | Calcium absorption | Rickets, osteomalacia | 400 IU |
| E | Antioxidant | Neurological symptoms | 10 mg |
| K | Coagulation | Bleeding disorders | 80 μg |

#### Vitamins (Water-soluble)
| Vitamin | Primary Function | Deficiency | RDA |
|---------|------------------|------------|-----|
| B1 (Thiamine) | Energy metabolism | Beriberi | 1.2 mg |
| B2 (Riboflavin) | Energy metabolism | Cheilosis | 1.3 mg |
| B3 (Niacin) | Energy metabolism | Pellagra | 16 mg |
| B6 (Pyridoxine) | Protein metabolism | Peripheral neuropathy | 1.3 mg |
| B9 (Folate) | DNA synthesis | Megaloblastic anemia | 400 μg |
| B12 (Cobalamin) | DNA synthesis | Pernicious anemia | 2.4 μg |
| C | Antioxidant | Scurvy | 75 mg |

#### Minerals
| Mineral | Function | Deficiency | RDA |
|---------|----------|------------|-----|
| Iron | Hemoglobin synthesis | Iron deficiency anemia | 8-18 mg |
| Calcium | Bone health | Osteoporosis | 1000 mg |
| Iodine | Thyroid function | Goiter, cretinism | 150 μg |
| Zinc | Growth, immunity | Growth retardation | 11 mg |
| Selenium | Antioxidant | Keshan disease | 55 μg |

### 3. Caloric Requirements
- **Adult males**: 2400-2800 kcal/day
- **Adult females**: 2000-2200 kcal/day
- **Children**: 1000-2000 kcal/day
- **Pregnant women**: +300 kcal/day
- **Lactating women**: +500 kcal/day

**Formula**: Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)
- Males: BMR = 66 + (13.7 × W) + (5 × H) - (6.8 × A)
- Females: BMR = 655 + (9.6 × W) + (1.8 × H) - (4.7 × A)
Where: W=weight(kg), H=height(cm), A=age(years)

### 4. Balanced Diet Principles
1. **Proportional**: All nutrients in right proportions
2. **Adequate**: Sufficient quantity for needs
3. **Safe**: Free from harmful substances
4. **Acceptable**: Culturally and economically feasible

### 5. Recommended Dietary Allowances (RDA) - India 2020
**Quick Reference**:
- Cereals: 300-400 g/day
- Pulses: 40-60 g/day
- Milk: 200-300 ml/day
- Vegetables: 300-400 g/day
- Fruits: 100-150 g/day
- Oil: 20-30 g/day

## Section B: Protein-Energy Malnutrition

### 1. Types of PEM

#### Marasmus
**Clinical Features**:
- Severe wasting (<60% weight for age)
- "Matchstick" limbs
- "Old man" facies
- Loss of subcutaneous fat
- Normal hair texture
- Preserved serum albumin

**Pathophysiology**: Inadequate calorie intake → Fat and muscle depletion

#### Kwashiorkor
**Clinical Features**:
- Edema (pedal, facial)
- "Flaky paint" dermatosis
- "Flag sign" in hair
- Hepatomegaly (fatty liver)
- Apathy and irritability
- Decreased serum albumin

**Pathophysiology**: Inadequate protein intake → Decreased oncotic pressure

**Memory Aid**: "MARA" - Marasmus: Atrophy, No edema; "KWASH" - Kwashiorkor: Edema, Albumin low

### 2. Anthropometric Measurements

#### Weight-for-Age
- **Normal**: 80-120% of expected
- **Grade I PEM**: 70-79% (Mild)
- **Grade II PEM**: 60-69% (Moderate)
- **Grade III PEM**: <60% (Severe)

#### Height-for-Age
- **Normal**: >95% of expected
- **Stunting**: <90% (Chronic malnutrition)

#### Weight-for-Height
- **Normal**: 90-110% of expected
- **Wasting**: <80% (Acute malnutrition)

#### Mid-Upper Arm Circumference (MUAC)
- **6-59 months**: 
  - Normal: ≥12.5 cm
  - At risk: 12.0-12.4 cm
  - SAM: <11.5 cm

### 3. Assessment Methods

#### Indian Academy of Pediatrics (IAP) Classification

**Weight for Age (Gomez Classification)**:
- Grade I: 75-90% of expected
- Grade II: 60-74% of expected  
- Grade III: <60% of expected

**Waterlow Classification**:
- Combines stunting and wasting for comprehensive assessment

#### WHO Child Growth Standards (2006)
- Z-score approach
- <-2 SD: Undernutrition
- <-3 SD: Severe undernutrition

### 4. Prevention Strategies

#### Primary Prevention
1. **Breastfeeding**: Exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months
2. **Complementary feeding**: At 6 months with continuation of breastfeeding
3. **Food security**: Access to adequate food
4. **Health education**: Proper feeding practices

#### Secondary Prevention
1. **Growth monitoring**: Regular weight and height measurements
2. **Nutritional surveillance**: Community-based monitoring
3. **Early detection**: Community health workers

### 5. Community-Based Management

#### Management Protocol
**Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM)**:
- **Complicated SAM**: Inpatient treatment
- **Uncomplicated SAM**: Outpatient treatment with RUTF

**Moderate Acute Malnutrition (MAM)**:
- **Outpatient management**
- **Ready-to-use supplementary food (RUSF)**

#### Therapeutic Foods
**RUTF (Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food)**:
- 500-550 kcal/100g
- Ingredients: Peanut paste, milk powder, sugar, oil, minerals
- No water required

**RUSF (Ready-to-Use Supplementary Food)**:
- 400-450 kcal/100g
- For moderate malnutrition

## Section C: Vitamin Deficiencies

### 1. Vitamin A Deficiency

#### Prevalence (NFHS-5)
- Night blindness: 12.2% children, 6.9% women
- Bitot's spots: 0.7% children

#### Clinical Manifestations
**Ocular**:
- Night blindness (first symptom)
- Conjunctival xerosis
- Bitot's spots
- Corneal xerosis
- Keratomalacia

**Systemic**:
- Increased susceptibility to infections
- Impaired growth
- Increased mortality risk

#### WHO Classification
**XN**: Night blindness
**X1A**: Conjunctival xerosis
**X1B**: Bitot's spots
**X2**: Corneal xerosis
**X3A**: Corneal ulceration/keratomalacia <1/3 cornea
**X3B**: Corneal ulceration/keratomalacia ≥1/3 cornea
**XS**: Corneal scar
**XF**: Xerophthalmia fundus

#### Prevention and Control
**Universal supplementation**:
- Children 6-59 months: 100,000 IU (6-12 months), 200,000 IU (12-59 months)
- Postpartum women: 200,000 IU within 6 weeks of delivery
- Pregnant women: 10,000 IU daily or 25,000 IU weekly (safe in pregnancy)

**Food-based approaches**: Dark green leafy vegetables, orange fruits/vegetables

### 2. Vitamin D Deficiency

#### Risk Factors
- Inadequate sunlight exposure
- Dark skin pigmentation
- Urban living
- Air pollution
- Dress code covering most body

#### Clinical Manifestations
**Rickets (Children)**:
- Delayed fontanelle closure
- Rachitic rosary
- Harrison's sulcus
- Bow legs/knock knees
- Delayed tooth eruption

**Osteomalacia (Adults)**:
- Bone pain
- Muscle weakness
- Pathological fractures

#### Treatment
**Children**: 
- Vitamin D 2000-5000 IU/day for 6-12 weeks
- Maintenance: 400 IU/day

**Adults**:
- Vitamin D 60,000 IU/week for 8 weeks
- Maintenance: 1000 IU/day

### 3. B-Complex Deficiencies

#### Thiamine (B1) Deficiency - Beriberi
**Clinical Types**:
- **Dry beriberi**: Peripheral neuropathy, muscle wasting
- **Wet beriberi**: High-output cardiac failure, edema
- **Wernicke-Korsakoff**: Ataxia, confusion, memory loss

**Risk factors**: Alcoholism, polished rice diet, chronic diarrhea

**Treatment**: Thiamine 100mg IV/IM for 1-2 weeks, then oral 50mg daily

#### Riboflavin (B2) Deficiency
**Clinical features**:
- Cheilosis (cracked lips)
- Angular stomatitis
- Glossitis
- Photophobia

#### Niacin (B3) Deficiency - Pellagra
**"3 D's"**:
1. **Dermatitis**: Symmetric, pigmented rash (Casal's necklace)
2. **Diarrhea**: Malabsorption, inflammation
3. **Dementia**: Confusion, hallucinations

**Treatment**: Niacin 100-300 mg daily

#### Pyridoxine (B6) Deficiency
**Clinical features**:
- Peripheral neuropathy
- Convulsions (infants)
- Sideroblastic anemia
- Cheilosis

#### Folate (B9) Deficiency
**Clinical features**:
- Megaloblastic anemia
- Neural tube defects (pregnancy)
- Glossitis

**Treatment**: Folic acid 1-5 mg daily

#### Cobalamin (B12) Deficiency
**Clinical features**:
- Megaloblastic anemia
- Peripheral neuropathy
- Subacute combined degeneration of spinal cord
- Cognitive impairment

**Risk factors**: Vegan diet, pernicious anemia, malabsorption

**Treatment**: B12 1000 μg IM daily × 1 week, then weekly × 1 month, then monthly

### 4. Vitamin C Deficiency - Scurvy

#### Clinical Manifestations
- Fatigue and weakness
- Joint and muscle pain
- Bleeding gums
- Petechiae and ecchymoses
- Poor wound healing
- Corkscrew hairs

**Diagnosis**: Plasma vitamin C <11.4 μmol/L

**Treatment**: Vitamin C 100-300 mg daily for 1-2 weeks

### 5. Iron Deficiency Anemia

#### Prevalence (NFHS-5)
- Children 6-59 months: 67.1%
- Women 15-49 years: 57.2%
- Pregnant women: 52.2%

#### Risk Factors
- Rapid growth phases
- Menstruation
- Pregnancy and lactation
- Inadequate dietary iron
- Parasitic infections
- Malaria

#### Clinical Features
- Pallor (conjunctival, palmar)
- Fatigue and weakness
- Dyspnea on exertion
- Pica (craving for ice, dirt, etc.)
- Koilonychia (spoon nails)

#### Laboratory Diagnosis
**Iron studies**:
- Hemoglobin: <11 g/dL (children), <12 g/dL (women)
- MCV: <80 fL (microcytic)
- Serum ferritin: <15 μg/L
- Serum iron: <50 μg/dL
- TIBC: >350 μg/dL
- Transferrin saturation: <16%

#### Treatment Protocol
**Children**: 
- Iron: 2-3 mg/kg/day elemental iron
- Duration: 3 months after hemoglobin normalizes

**Adults**:
- Iron: 100-200 mg elemental iron daily
- Duration: 3-6 months

**Pregnant women**: 
- Iron: 60 mg elemental iron + 400 μg folic acid

### 6. Iodine Deficiency Disorders

#### Prevalence (NFHS-5)
- Goiter rate: 24.6% (mild to severe)
- Total affected population: >200 million

#### Clinical Spectrum
**Thyroid enlargement**: Goiter (compensatory hyperplasia)
**Hypothyroidism**: 
- Cretinism (irreversible mental retardation)
- Delayed development
- Deaf-mutism
- Motor spasticity

**Neonatal hypothyroidism**: 
- Increased neonatal mortality
- Mental retardation
- Growth retardation

#### Prevention Strategy
**Universal Salt Iodization**: 
- Iodine content: 15-40 ppm potassium iodate
- Salt specifications: >95% of households use iodized salt
- Monitoring: Salt testing kits

**Coverage (NFHS-5)**: 85.6% households use iodized salt

### 7. Zinc Deficiency

#### Clinical Features
- Growth retardation
- Delayed sexual maturation
- Alopecia
- Dermatitis
- Delayed wound healing
- Impaired immunity
- Diarrhea

#### Risk Factors
- Inadequate dietary zinc
- Malabsorption syndromes
- Chronic liver/kidney disease
- Alcoholism

#### Treatment: Elemental zinc 1-2 mg/kg/day

## Section D: Nutrition in Special Groups

### 1. Infant and Child Nutrition

#### Breastfeeding
**WHO recommendations**:
- Exclusive breastfeeding: 0-6 months
- Continued breastfeeding: Up to 2 years with complementary foods

**Advantages**:
- Optimal nutrition
- Protection against infections
- Mother-child bonding
- Natural contraception

**Contraindications**:
- HIV positive mother (resource-rich settings)
- Active TB
- HSV lesions on breast
- Galactosemia in infant
- Certain medications

#### Complementary Feeding (6-24 months)
**Principles**:
- Timely introduction (6 months)
- Adequate frequency and variety
- Minimize spices initially
- Continue breastfeeding

**Food groups**:
- Cereals and millets
- Pulses and legumes
- Milk and milk products
- Vegetables and fruits
- Fats and oils

**Age-specific feeding**:
- 6-8 months: 2-3 meals + breastfeeding
- 9-11 months: 3-4 meals + 1-2 snacks + breastfeeding
- 12-24 months: 4-5 meals + 1-2 snacks + breastfeeding

#### Growth Monitoring
**Parameters**: Weight, height, head circumference
**Frequency**: 
- Under 1 year: Monthly
- 1-3 years: Every 2-3 months
- Above 3 years: Every 6 months

### 2. Adolescent Nutrition

#### Nutritional Requirements
**Increased needs**:
- Energy: 2200-2500 kcal/day (boys), 1800-2200 kcal/day (girls)
- Protein: 0.85-0.9 g/kg body weight
- Iron: 15 mg/day (boys), 25 mg/day (girls)
- Calcium: 1300 mg/day

#### Special Issues
**Iron deficiency anemia**: High prevalence due to growth spurt
**Undernutrition**: Stunting, wasting
**Overnutrition**: Rising prevalence of obesity
**Eating disorders**: Anorexia, bulimia

### 3. Maternal Nutrition

#### Preconception
**Folic acid**: 400 μg daily to prevent neural tube defects
**Iron**: Treat existing anemia
**Weight**: Achieve normal BMI

#### Pregnancy
**Weight gain recommendations**:
- Underweight (BMI <18.5): 12.5-18 kg
- Normal (BMI 18.5-24.9): 11.5-16 kg
- Overweight (BMI 25-29.9): 7-11.5 kg
- Obese (BMI ≥30): 5-9 kg

**Nutrient requirements**:
- Energy: +300 kcal/day
- Protein: +1.3 g/kg/day
- Iron: 27 mg/day
- Folic acid: 600 μg/day
- Calcium: 1000 mg/day

**Critical periods**:
- Neural tube closure: 3-4 weeks post-conception
- Brain development: Throughout pregnancy
- Weight gain: Variable pattern

#### Lactation
**Energy requirements**: +500 kcal/day
**Protein**: +1.8 g/kg/day
**Fluid**: 2.5-3 liters/day
**Nutrient-dense foods**: For quality breast milk

### 4. Geriatric Nutrition

#### Age-related Changes
**Physiological**:
- Decreased basal metabolic rate
- Reduced taste and smell
- Decreased absorption
- Reduced renal function
- Increased chronic diseases

#### Nutritional Requirements
**Energy**: 10-20% reduction with age
**Protein**: 1.0-1.2 g/kg body weight
**Calcium**: 1200 mg/day (osteoporosis prevention)
**Vitamin D**: 800-1000 IU/day
**Fiber**: 25-30 g/day
**Fluids**: 30 ml/kg body weight

#### Common Problems
**Malnutrition**: 15-20% prevalence
**Osteoporosis**: Post-menopausal women
**Sarcopenia**: Age-related muscle loss
**Dehydration**: Reduced thirst sensation

### 5. Sports Nutrition

#### Energy Requirements
**Endurance sports**: 50-60 kcal/kg body weight
**Strength sports**: 40-50 kcal/kg body weight
**Mixed sports**: 45-55 kcal/kg body weight

#### Macronutrient Distribution
**Carbohydrates**: 55-65% of total energy
**Proteins**: 12-15% of total energy
**Fats**: 20-30% of total energy

#### Timing
**Pre-exercise**: Carbohydrates 2-3 hours before
**During exercise**: Fluids every 15-20 minutes
**Post-exercise**: Carbohydrates + protein within 30 minutes

## Section E: Food Safety and Security

### 1. Food Security in India

#### Definition
**Food security**: Physical, economic and social access to sufficient, safe and nutritious food

**Dimensions**:
1. **Availability**: Food production and imports
2. **Access**: Economic and physical access
3. **Utilization**: Nutritional value and health status
4. **Stability**: Food security over time

#### Current Status (NFHS-5)
**Food consumption**:
- Rice/wheat: 71.1% households
- Pulses: 32.5% households daily
- Vegetables: 36.8% households daily
- Fruits: 9.2% households daily

**Nutritional status**:
- Stunting: 35.5% children under 5
- Wasting: 19.3% children under 5
- Underweight: 32.1% children under 5
- Anemia: 67.1% children, 57.2% women

### 2. Public Distribution System (PDS)

#### Structure
**Central Government**:
- Procurement of food grains
- Allocation to states
- Policy formulation

**State Governments**:
- Distribution to Fair Price Shops
- Implementation of programs
- Identification of beneficiaries

#### Food Corporation of India (FCI)
**Functions**:
- Procurement of food grains
- Storage and transport
- Distribution to states

#### Coverage and Targeting
**Universal PDS**: Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Chandigarh
**Targeted PPDS**: Rest of India

**Food grains allocation**:
- Rice: ₹3/kg (AAY), ₹7/kg (APL)
- Wheat: ₹2/kg (AAY), ₹7/kg (APL)

### 3. Nutrition Surveillance

#### National Family Health Survey (NFHS)
**Conducted by**: International Institute for Population Sciences (IIPS)
**Frequency**: Every 5 years
**Coverage**: All states and UTs

**NFHS-5 (2019-21) key findings**:
- Stunting: 35.5% (↓ from 38.4%)
- Wasting: 19.3% (↑ from 21.0%)
- Underweight: 32.1% (↓ from 35.7%)
- Anemia: 67.1% children, 57.2% women

#### District Level Nutrition Survey (DLNS)
**Conducted by**: UNICEF and RD&FW Department
**Focus**: 100 districts with high malnutrition
**Parameters**: Anthropometric, dietary, clinical assessment

### 4. Food Fortification Programs

#### Fortification Strategy
**Rationale**: Address micronutrient deficiencies in population
**Cost-effective**: Reaches large populations
**Technology**: Heat-stable vitamins and minerals

#### National Programme
**Double Fortified Salt (DFS)**:
- Fortified with iodine + iron
- Target: Control iron deficiency anemia
- Coverage: 10 states

**Wheat Flour Fortification**:
- Fortified with iron, folic acid, vitamin B12
- Production-linked incentives for millers
- Target: 10% of wheat flour market

**Oil Fortification**:
- Fortified with vitamin A and D
- Oil industry partnership
- Target: Large oil companies

**Milk Fortification**:
- Fortified with vitamin A and D
- Co-op and private dairy companies
- Target: Urban milk market

### 5. Food Adulteration and Safety

#### Common Adulterants
**Milk**: Water, skim milk powder, urea, formaldehyde
**Ghee/Butter**: Vanaspati, animal fat
**Spices**: Colored saw dust, brick powder
**Honey**: Sugar syrup, jaggery
**Wheat flour**: Chalk powder, sand
**Coffee**: Roasted barley, dates
**Tea**: Colored leaves, dried leaves

#### Prevention Strategies
**FSSAI (Food Safety and Standards Authority of India)**:
- Food safety regulations
- Certification of food businesses
- Testing laboratories

**Consumer awareness**:
- Simple tests (purity tests)
- Complaint mechanisms
- Consumer courts

## Anthropometric Charts and Calculations

### 1. Weight-for-Age Charts

#### Indian Academy of Pediatrics (IAP) Standards
**Gomez Classification**:
- Normal: >90% expected weight
- Grade I PEM: 75-90% expected weight
- Grade II PEM: 60-74% expected weight
- Grade III PEM: <60% expected weight

**Expected Weight Calculation**:
- **0-1 year**: Expected weight = (Age in months × 0.5) + 4 kg
- **1-6 years**: Expected weight = (Age in years × 2) + 8 kg

### 2. Height-for-Age Assessment

#### Stunting Classification
- **Normal**: >95% expected height
- **Moderate stunting**: 90-94% expected height
- **Severe stunting**: <90% expected height

**Expected Height Calculation**:
- **2-12 years**: Expected height = (Age in years × 6) + 77 cm

### 3. Body Mass Index (BMI)

#### Adult BMI Categories
- **Underweight**: <18.5 kg/m²
- **Normal**: 18.5-24.9 kg/m²
- **Overweight**: 25-29.9 kg/m²
- **Obese**: ≥30 kg/m²

**BMI Calculation**: BMI = Weight (kg) / Height² (m²)

#### Children BMI-for-Age
- **Underweight**: <5th percentile
- **Normal**: 5th-85th percentile
- **Overweight**: 85th-95th percentile
- **Obese**: ≥95th percentile

### 4. Mid-Upper Arm Circumference (MUAC)

#### Age-Specific Cutoffs (6-59 months)
- **Severe acute malnutrition**: <11.5 cm
- **Moderate acute malnutrition**: 11.5-12.4 cm
- **Normal**: ≥12.5 cm

**Adult Cutoffs**:
- **Severe acute malnutrition**: <21 cm (women), <22 cm (men)
- **Moderate acute malnutrition**: 21-22.9 cm (women), 22-23.9 cm (men)

### 5. Z-Score Calculations

#### Formula
**Z-score = (Observed value - Median value) / Standard Deviation**

#### Interpretation
- **Normal**: Z > -2
- **At risk**: -2 ≥ Z > -3
- **Severely malnourished**: Z ≤ -3

### 6. Growth Velocity Assessment

#### Weight Velocity
**Critical periods**:
- **Birth to 1 year**: Rapid weight gain
- **1-2 years**: Slower growth
- **2-5 years**: Steady growth
- **School age**: Slower until puberty

## Clinical Case Studies

### Case 1: Protein-Energy Malnutrition
**Patient**: 18-month-old child, weight 6 kg
**History**: Poor appetite for 3 months, lethargy, frequent infections
**Examination**: Wasted appearance, "matchstick" limbs, normal hair and skin

**Questions**:
1. What is the nutritional diagnosis?
2. Calculate expected weight for age and classify PEM grade
3. What are the immediate management priorities?

**Answers**:
1. **Diagnosis**: Marasmus (Grade III PEM)
2. **Expected weight**: (1.5 × 2) + 8 = 11 kg; Actual = 6 kg = 54.5% → Grade III PEM
3. **Management**: 
   - Inpatient care initially
   - Therapeutic feeding with RUTF
   - Treat complications (infections)
   - Gradual rehydration
   - Family counseling

### Case 2: Vitamin A Deficiency
**Patient**: 5-year-old child, night blindness for 1 week
**History**: Poor diet, mother notices child cannot see in dim light
**Examination**: Dry conjunctiva, Bitot's spots present

**Questions**:
1. What is the clinical diagnosis?
2. What is the WHO classification?
3. What is the treatment protocol?

**Answers**:
1. **Diagnosis**: Vitamin A deficiency - XN + X1B
2. **Classification**: Night blindness (XN) with Bitot's spots (X1B)
3. **Treatment**: 
   - Vitamin A: 200,000 IU (1 capsule) immediately
   - Repeat dose next day
   - Third dose after 2 weeks
   - Counseling on dietary sources

### Case 3: Iron Deficiency Anemia
**Patient**: 25-year-old female, fatigue for 3 months
**History**: Heavy menstrual bleeding, poor diet
**Examination**: Pallor, koilonychia present
**Labs**: Hb 8.2 g/dL, MCV 72 fL, serum ferritin 8 μg/L

**Questions**:
1. What is the diagnosis?
2. Calculate the TIBC and serum iron
3. What is the treatment protocol?

**Answers**:
1. **Diagnosis**: Iron deficiency anemia
2. **Expected values**: 
   - TIBC >350 μg/dL (estimated 420 μg/dL)
   - Serum iron <50 μg/dL (estimated 35 μg/dL)
3. **Treatment**: 
   - Elemental iron: 200 mg/day
   - Duration: 3-6 months (3 months after Hb normalizes)
   - Address underlying cause (menstrual bleeding)

### Case 4: Iodine Deficiency Disorder
**Patient**: 8-year-old child, enlarged thyroid, poor school performance
**History**: From endemic goiter area, mother's goiter
**Examination**: Diffuse goiter grade II, delayed developmental milestones
**Labs**: TSH elevated, T3/T4 low

**Questions**:
1. What is the diagnosis?
2. What are the preventive measures?
3. How to prevent recurrence?

**Answers**:
1. **Diagnosis**: Iodine deficiency disorder with endemic goiter and mild cretinism
2. **Prevention**: 
   - Universal salt iodization (15-40 ppm KI)
   - Public awareness
   - Regular monitoring
3. **Management**: 
   - Thyroid hormone replacement
   - Educare support
   - Lifelong iodized salt use

## Summary and Key Takeaways

### Critical Concepts for Examinations
1. **PEM Classification**: Weight-for-age is most sensitive for community surveys
2. **WHO Growth Standards**: Z-score approach preferred over percentage
3. **Vitamin A**: Night blindness is earliest symptom, correct with supplementation
4. **Iron Deficiency**: Ferritin <15 μg/L is diagnostic, treat for 3 months after normalization
5. **Iodine Deficiency**: Universal salt iodization at 15-40 ppm potassium iodate
6. **NFHS-5**: Stunting 35.5%, wasting 19.3% - still major public health problem
7. **POSHAN 2.0**: Nutrition Mission 2.0 with convergence approach

### High-Yield Facts
- **Exclusive breastfeeding**: 0-6 months, reduces infant mortality by 13%
- **Complementary feeding**: Start at 6 months with continued breastfeeding
- **RUTF**: 500-550 kcal/100g, no water required
- **Anemia prevalence**: 67.1% children, 57.2% women (NFHS-5)
- **Salt iodization coverage**: 85.6% households (NFHS-5)
- **PDS beneficiaries**: 80% of rural households

### Common MCQ Pitfalls
- Confusing marasmus with kwashiorkor
- Wrong calculation of expected weight/height
- Mixing vitamin deficiency symptoms
- Incorrect hemoglobin cutoffs for different population groups
- Not knowing current NFHS-5 statistics

### Essential Formulas
- **Expected weight (0-1 yr)**: (Age in months × 0.5) + 4 kg
- **Expected weight (1-6 yr)**: (Age in years × 2) + 8 kg
- **Expected height (2-12 yr)**: (Age in years × 6) + 77 cm
- **BMI**: Weight (kg) / Height² (m²)
- **Z-score**: (Observed - Median) / SD
- **BMR (Males)**: 66 + (13.7 × W) + (5 × H) - (6.8 × A)
