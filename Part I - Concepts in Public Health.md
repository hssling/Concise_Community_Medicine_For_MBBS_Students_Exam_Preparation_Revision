### üèõÔ∏è KEY MILESTONES IN MEDICAL HISTORY

#### Pre-Historic & Ancient Period (3000 BCE - 500 CE)
- **Egyptian Medicine**: Papyrus Ebers (1550 BCE) - First medical texts
- **Hippocrates** (460-370 BCE): Father of Medicine, Hippocratic Oath
- **Galen** (130-200 CE): Roman physician, anatomical studies
- **Sushruta** (600 BCE): Father of Surgery, Indian medical system

#### Medieval Period (500-1500 CE)
- **Islamic Medicine**: Avicenna's Canon of Medicine
- **Medical Education**: First universities (Bologna, Salerno)
- **Black Death (1347-1351)**: Catalyzed public health measures

#### Renaissance Period (1500-1700)
- **Andreas Vesalius**: Modern anatomy (1543)
- **William Harvey**: Blood circulation (1628)
- **Paracelsus**: Chemical medicine

#### 18th Century - Public Health Emergence
- **John Snow (1854)**: Cholera outbreak investigation, Epidemiology birth
- **Edwin Chadwick**: Sanitary movement in England
- **Jenner**: Smallpox vaccination (1798)

#### 19th Century - Scientific Medicine
- **Louis Pasteur**: Germ theory (1860s)
- **Robert Koch**: Koch's postulates (1882)
- **Joseph Lister**: Antiseptic surgery (1867)
- **Florence Nightingale**: Modern nursing, hospital sanitation

#### 20th Century - Preventive Medicine Revolution
- **Alexander Fleming**: Penicillin discovery (1928)
- **Jonas Salk**: Polio vaccine (1955)
- **WHO Founded**: 1948
- **Primary Health Care**: Alma-Ata Declaration (1978)

## Chapter 3: Preventive Medicine

### üéØ QUICK FACTS BOX
- **Definition**: "The art and science of preventing disease, promoting health, and prolonging life"
- **Scope**: Individual, family, community, population levels
- **Approaches**: Health promotion, disease prevention, health protection
- **Evidence Base**: Epidemiology, biostatistics, behavioral sciences

### üéØ DEFINITION & CONCEPTS

#### Classical Definition (Leavell & Clark, 1965)
> "Preventive medicine comprises measures designed to protect the health of populations and to promote the well-being of people"

#### Modern Definition (WHO, 2023)
> "All organized measures to prevent disease, promote health, and prolong life among the population as a whole"

#### Core Principles
1. **Primordial Prevention**: Preventing emergence of risk factors
2. **Primary Prevention**: Preventing disease before onset
3. **Secondary Prevention**: Early detection and treatment
4. **Tertiary Prevention**: Reducing complications and disability

### üèîÔ∏è LEVELS OF PREVENTION

#### PRIMARY PREVENTION
**Definition**: Interventions applied before disease occurs

**Types:**
- **Health Promotion**: Education, lifestyle modification
- **Specific Protection**: Immunization, sanitation, safety measures

**Examples:**
- Health education programs
- Vaccination campaigns
- Water fluoridation
- Smoke-free legislation
- Safe sex education
- Nutrition supplementation

**Strategies:**
- Population strategy: Mass interventions
- High-risk strategy: Targeting susceptible groups
- Individual strategy: Personalized interventions

#### SECONDARY PREVENTION
**Definition**: Early detection and prompt treatment to prevent complications

**Methods:**
- **Screening**: Mass screening, selective screening
- **Case finding**: Opportunistic screening
- **Early treatment**: Immediate intervention

**Examples:**
- **Infectious diseases**: Contact tracing, outbreak investigation
- **NCDs**: BP screening, diabetes testing
- **Cancers**: Pap smear, mammography
- **Mental health**: Depression screening

**Screening Criteria (Wilson-Jungner):**
1. Condition is important health problem
2. Accepted treatment available
3. Facilities for diagnosis and treatment
4. Latent stage identifiable
5. Natural history understood
6. Suitable screening test available
7. Test acceptable to population
8. Adequate follow-up system
9. Cost-effective intervention
10. Continuous process

#### TERTIARY PREVENTION
**Definition**: Reducing complications and disabilities after disease manifestation

**Objectives:**
- Prevent disease progression
- Minimize disability
- Restore function
- Improve quality of life

**Methods:**
- **Disability limitation**: Treatment to prevent complications
- **Rehabilitation**: Physical, occupational, social rehabilitation

**Examples:**
- **Diabetes**: Preventing complications, foot care
- **Tuberculosis**: DOTS for drug resistance prevention
- **Stroke**: Rehabilitation programs
- **Mental illness**: Community integration programs

#### PRIMORDIAL PREVENTION
**Definition**: Preventing development of risk factors

**Focus**: Social, economic, environmental factors

**Examples:**
- Preventing childhood obesity
- Preventing hypertension in children
- Preventing tobacco use initiation
- Preventing alcohol abuse in youth

### üìà NATURAL HISTORY OF DISEASE

#### Natural History Model (Leavell & Clark)

**Pre-pathogenesis Phase:**
- **Host factors**: Age, sex, genetics, immunity, nutrition
- **Agent factors**: Infectious agents, chemical, physical, nutritional
- **Environmental factors**: Physical, biological, social environment

**Pathogenesis Phase:**
1. **Subclinical disease**: No symptoms, detectable by tests
2. **Clinical disease**: Symptoms and signs appear
3. **Recovery/Disability/Death**: Disease outcomes

#### Levels of Prevention Application

| Phase | Level | Intervention Examples |
|-------|-------|---------------------|
| **Pre-pathogenesis** | Primordial/Primary | Health education, immunization |
| **Early pathogenesis** | Primary | Early treatment, screening |
| **Subclinical** | Secondary | Screening programs |
| **Clinical** | Secondary | Early treatment |
| **Late stages** | Tertiary | Rehabilitation, palliative care |

### ‚öñÔ∏è RISK FACTORS & RISK ASSESSMENT

#### Types of Risk Factors
1. **Biological**: Age, sex, genetics, physiology
2. **Environmental**: Air pollution, water quality, housing
3. **Behavioral**: Smoking, diet, exercise, alcohol
4. **Social**: Education, income, occupation, culture

#### Risk Assessment Components
- **Risk identification**: Recognizing potential hazards
- **Risk estimation**: Probability and magnitude
- **Risk evaluation**: Acceptability assessment
- **Risk management**: Intervention strategies

#### Risk Communication
- Clear, understandable information
- Honest assessment of uncertainties
- Empowering individuals for decision-making

## Chapter 4: Social Sciences

### üéØ QUICK FACTS BOX
- **Social Determinants**: 30-55% contribution to health outcomes
- **Health Inequality**: Rich-poor gap in health indicators
- **Social Gradient**: Health varies by socioeconomic status
- **Social Cohesion**: Community support impacts health

### üèòÔ∏è SOCIAL DETERMINANTS OF HEALTH

#### WHO Commission on Social Determinants (2005-2008)

**Structural Determinants:**
1. **Socioeconomic Position**
   - Income and wealth distribution
   - Education levels
   - Occupation and employment
   - Social class

2. **Political Context**
   - Governance quality
   - Public policy priorities
   - Social protection systems
   - Human rights frameworks

**Intermediary Determinants:**
1. **Material Circumstances**
   - Housing quality
   - Working conditions
   - Neighborhood safety
   - Access to services

2. **Psychosocial Factors**
   - Social support networks
   - Stress levels
   - Control and autonomy
   - Social cohesion

3. **Behavioral Factors**
   - Individual lifestyle choices
   - Smoking, alcohol, diet
   - Physical activity patterns
   - Healthcare utilization

#### Social Gradient in Health
- **Social Determinants Gradient**: Health improves progressively with higher socioeconomic position
- **Income-Health Relationship**: Each income quintile shows better health than lower quintile
- **Not just poor vs. rich**: Continuous gradient throughout society

### üåç CULTURAL FACTORS IN HEALTH

#### Culture Definition
> "The learned and shared patterns of behavior, beliefs, and values that characterize groups of people"

#### Components of Culture
1. **Beliefs**: About health, disease, treatment
2. **Practices**: Traditional healing, rituals, customs
3. **Values**: Health priorities, family roles
4. **Knowledge**: Health information sources
5. **Language**: Health communication barriers

#### Cultural Influence on Health
- **Health-seeking behavior**: Traditional vs. modern medicine
- **Dietary practices**: Religious food restrictions
- **Family roles**: Decision-making patterns
- **Communication**: Direct vs. indirect styles
- **Beliefs about causation**: Supernatural vs. scientific

#### Cultural Competence in Healthcare
1. **Cultural awareness**: Understanding own cultural biases
2. **Cultural knowledge**: Learning about patient cultures
3. **Cultural skills**: Adapting communication styles
4. **Cultural encounters**: Direct patient interactions
5. **Cultural desire**: Motivation to be culturally competent

#### Ethnocentrism vs. Cultural Relativism
- **Ethnocentrism**: Judging other cultures by own standards
- **Cultural Relativism**: Understanding practices within cultural context
- **Balanced approach**: Respect cultural diversity while promoting health

### üß† BEHAVIORAL SCIENCES IN MEDICINE

#### Health Behavior Models

**Health Belief Model (Rosenstock, 1966)**
**Components:**
- **Perceived susceptibility**: Risk of getting disease
- **Perceived severity**: Seriousness of consequences
- **Perceived benefits**: Effectiveness of action
- **Perceived barriers**: Obstacles to action
- **Cues to action**: Triggers for behavior
- **Self-efficacy**: Confidence in ability

**Theory of Planned Behavior (Ajzen, 1991)**
**Components:**
- **Attitude toward behavior**: Overall evaluation
- **Subjective norms**: Social pressure perception
- **Perceived behavioral control**: Control beliefs
- **Behavioral intention**: Motivation to perform
- **Actual behavior**: Performance of action

**Social Cognitive Theory (Bandura, 1986)**
**Key Concepts:**
- **Reciprocal determinism**: Interaction of person, behavior, environment
- **Observational learning**: Learning from others
- **Self-efficacy**: Belief in capabilities
- **Self-regulation**: Self-monitoring and evaluation

#### Behavioral Risk Factors

**Modifiable Risk Factors:**
- **Tobacco use**: Direct and secondhand smoke
- **Alcohol consumption**: Harmful use patterns
- **Physical inactivity**: Sedentary lifestyle
- **Unhealthy diet**: Excess calories, poor nutrition
- **Unsafe sex**: Multiple partners, no protection

**Non-modifiable Risk Factors:**
- **Age**: Increasing risk with age
- **Sex**: Gender-specific patterns
- **Genetics**: Family history influence
- **Ethnicity**: Cultural and genetic factors

### üë• COMMUNITY PARTICIPATION

#### Definition
> "The involvement of community members in decisions and actions that affect their health"

#### Levels of Participation (Arnstein's Ladder)
1. **Manipulation**: Pseudo-participation
2. **Tokenism**: Informing, consulting
3. **Citizen power**: Partnership, delegated power, citizen control

#### Benefits of Community Participation
- **Ownership**: Community commitment to programs
- **Sustainability**: Long-term program viability
- **Cultural relevance**: Culturally appropriate interventions
- **Cost-effectiveness**: Reduced resource requirements
- **Empowerment**: Community capacity building

#### Barriers to Community Participation
- **Lack of awareness**: About participation opportunities
- **Time constraints**: Competing priorities
- **Power imbalances**: Community vs. authority
- **Cultural barriers**: Gender, caste, language
- **Resource limitations**: Funding, facilities

#### Strategies for Effective Participation
1. **Community needs assessment**: Identify priorities
2. **Stakeholder mapping**: Identify key players
3. **Capacity building**: Skills and knowledge development
4. **Resource mobilization**: Local resource utilization
5. **Leadership development**: Community leaders
6. **Feedback mechanisms**: Continuous improvement

### üìä SOCIAL EPIDEMIOLOGY

#### Definition
> "The study of social distribution and social determinants of health states and events"

#### Core Concepts
1. **Social Distribution**: How health varies across social groups
2. **Social Causation**: Social factors cause health differences
3. **Social Selection**: Health affects social position
4. **Social Mobility**: Movement between social positions

#### Social Epidemiology Measures

**Social Class Indicators:**
- **Occupational class**: Job categories
- **Educational level**: Years of schooling
- **Income level**: Household income
- **Housing conditions**: Quality and ownership

**Health Inequalities Measures:**
- **Rate ratio**: Comparison between groups
- **Rate difference**: Absolute difference
- **Relative index of inequality**: Population impact
- **Slope index of inequality**: Gradient measure

#### Social Mobility and Health
- **Social causation**: Position affects health outcomes
- **Social selection**: Health affects position
- **Intergenerational effects**: Parental position influences child health
- **Life course perspective**: Cumulative effects over time
